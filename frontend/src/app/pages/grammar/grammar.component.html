이에요 / 예요

In Korean, 이에요 and 예요 are polite copula endings used to mean "to be" when stating identity or description. They attach directly to nouns. If the noun ends in a consonant, use 이에요. If it ends in a vowel, use 예요. This rule helps with pronunciation and natural flow in Korean sentences. These endings are informal polite and widely used in daily conversation, especially with people of similar or slightly higher status.

Examples:
책 (이에요) → 책이에요 ("It's a book"),
의사 (예요) → 의사예요 ("He/She is a doctor")

은 / 는

은 and 는 are topic particles in Korean grammar. They indicate what the sentence is about and often bring a nuance of contrast or emphasis. Use 은 after a noun that ends with a consonant, and 는 after a vowel. These particles are crucial for structuring information, especially when comparing two things or reintroducing known subjects. Their function is broader than the subject markers 이/가 and they are essential for understanding Korean discourse.

Examples:
저는 학생이에요 ("As for me, I am a student"),
날씨는 좋습니다 ("The weather is good – maybe unlike yesterday").

이 / 가

이 and 가 are subject particles that highlight the grammatical subject of a sentence. They help identify who or what is performing the action or being described. Use 이 after nouns ending in consonants and 가 after vowels. These particles are essential when introducing new information or focusing on the subject itself rather than the topic. Often, 이/가 is contrasted with 은/는, which is used for known or emphasized topics.

Examples:
사과가 맛있어요 ("The apple is delicious"),
친구가 왔어요 ("A friend came").

이 / 가 아니에요

To express negation ("not to be") in a polite way, Korean uses the structure 이/가 아니에요. Attach 이 아니에요 if the noun ends in a consonant and 가 아니에요 if it ends in a vowel. This form is used to politely deny identity or classification of something. It is important to match the right particle with the noun ending to maintain correct grammar and pronunciation.

Examples:
저는 학생이 아니에요 ("I am not a student"),
이것은 물건이 아니에요 ("This is not an object").
